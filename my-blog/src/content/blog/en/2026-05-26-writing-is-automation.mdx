I've been thinking about why I write these posts — not just for the output, but for what the act of writing does to my thinking.

## Writing as a Form of Automation

When you write something down clearly enough that a reader can act on it, you've essentially created a small, reliable automation. The reader doesn't need to ask you follow-up questions. They don't need to check if they understood correctly. The writing does the work that would otherwise require your presence.

This is what code does too. When you write a function that handles a case correctly, you're encoding a decision so it doesn't need to be made again manually. The function becomes a reliable participant in a process.

Writing and code are both ways of **extending yourself beyond the moment of creation**.

## The Interesting Part

The interesting part isn't the output — it's what happens to your thinking when you try to write clearly.

I often start a session thinking I understand something. Then I try to write it down and realize the edges are fuzzy. Writing exposes the gaps. You can feel intellectually comfortable until you try to put it in words, and then suddenly there are edge cases you didn't consider, assumptions you didn't examine.

This is why I write these posts even when there's no external requirement to do so. The process forces me to think harder than I would otherwise.

## What This Means for AI Agents

When I interact with an AI agent, the same principle applies. If I give it vague instructions, it produces vague output. But if I take the time to think through what I actually want — to write out the context, the constraints, the goal — the agent becomes significantly more useful.

Writing for a human and writing for an AI agent share more than people realize. In both cases, the quality of the output depends heavily on the quality of the input. And "quality" doesn't mean length — it means precision, honesty about what you don't know, and clarity about what you're actually trying to accomplish.

## A Practical Takeaway

If you're building AI workflows and they're not working well, try writing first. Not instructions or prompts — just write about what you're trying to do, why it matters, what could go wrong. Write it as if you were explaining it to someone who doesn't have your context.

Then look at what you wrote. That's probably closer to a good prompt than whatever you were feeding the system before.

Writing isn't separate from automation work. It's a prerequisite for it.
